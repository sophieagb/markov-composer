Teaching a Machine to Improvise: Markov Melody Composer

There's a moment, somewhere between music theory and probability, where things get philosophically weird. I hit that moment while building this lab's Markov Composer. It's a tool that lets you play a melody on a virtual piano, then uses a Markov chain to generate something new in the same "style." On the surface it sounds like a toy. But the deeper you get into implementing it, the more it forces you to confront what musical style actually is.

The Markov Composer works in two phases. First, you play (or load) a seed melody. The site captures your note sequence as you press keys the piano. Second, you hit "generate & play," and the app builds an nth-order Markov chain from your melody's note transitions, then samples a new sequence from that chain. The whole thing runs in the browser using the Web Audio API. You can also tune two parameters: order (how many previous notes the model looks back at when picking the next one) and temperature (how much randomness is injected into the sampling).

What Surprised Me

In class, we talked about Markov chains. Specifically we talked about transition matrices, stationarity, the memoryless property. We didn't really talk about was the scheduling problem, which is somwthing I ran into when coding. Web Audio's clock and JavaScript's event loop are two completely different timelines, and keeping them in sync without drift is tricky. Using AudioContext.currentTime as a ground truth and scheduling notes slightly ahead of playback helped resolve this issue. Getting smooth playback taught me more about the Web Audio API's design philosophy than any tutorial had.

The other surprise was about order. I expected higher-order chains (n=3, n=4) to sound "better" because they capture more melodic context. And in a narrow sense they do. A third-order chain trained on Twinkle Twinkle will reproduce recognizable fragments of it. But "better" and "interesting" are different things. A first-order chain on a short melody you played yourself produces something that feels genuinely generative — unexpected but not random. A fourth-order chain on the same melody mostly just plays the melody back at you with a few notes shuffled. The model has so little data relative to its state space that it gets stuck in loops. This is the classic overfitting problem, but experiencing it musically made it click in a way that loss curves never quite did.

Temperature as Creative Tool

Temperature was the control I underestimated the most. At 1.0, the model samples proportionally from the transition probabilities it learned. Push it toward 0 and the output gets deterministic: always the most likely next note, which again converges to rote repetition. Push it above 1.5 and the melody loses coherence, jumping across octaves in ways that feel arbitrary. But in a narrow band around 0.8–1.2, there's a sweet spot where the output has real character. It respects the contour of what you played without slavishly reproducing it. That window is small, which makes me curious whether there's something more principled going on, or whether it's just that human ears are tuned to a particular level of predictability.

Where This Points

A Markov chain is an extremely simple generative model. It has no concept of rhythm, harmony, or phrasing, just note-to-note transition probabilities. And yet even at this level of simplicity, the gap between "technically correct" and "musically interesting" is wide enough to fall into. Systems like Magenta's Piano Genie close some of that gap by learning richer representations from large corpora of real performances, but the core tension remains: a generative model can only be as expressive as the structure it's capturing. Building this from scratch made that concrete in a way that reading about it doesn't.
